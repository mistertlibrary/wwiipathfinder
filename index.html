US History II — Research Pathfinder

The Home Front A Research Guide to America and World War II

Five research pathways. Primary sources, curated collections, and essential reading on the decisions, injustices, and contributions that defined the American home front, 1941–1945.

Compiled by Mr. Thompson — School Library · US History II · 2025–2026

These five topics are presented separately for clarity, but they are not five separate stories. They are facets of a single, harder question: what does a democratic society do with the gap between its stated values and its actual conduct? The government that incarcerated Japanese Americans operated the same propaganda apparatus that largely excluded Black Americans from its mainstream recruitment campaigns — calculating, as it had calculated with the incarceration, that the political cost of Southern opposition outweighed the obligation to tell the full truth. The press that suppressed atomic bomb casualty reporting made many of the same editorial choices about Holocaust news. The women recruited into factories with one set of promises were sent home with another. Researching any one of these topics well will eventually lead you into the others.

Source types: Primary Secondary Curated Collection Longform Reference
Primary Source — Created at the time of the event: government documents, diaries, letters, newspapers, oral histories, photographs. Direct evidence; always carries the perspective and limits of its creator.
Secondary Source — Created after the event by someone interpreting primary sources: scholarly articles, museum overviews, encyclopedia entries. Gives context and analysis; carries the interpretive choices of its author.
Curated Collection — Primary or secondary sources selected and organized by a library, archive, or institution. Curation is interpretation: ask what the collector chose to include or omit.
Longform — A substantial essay or work of narrative journalism synthesizing evidence for a general educated audience. Often by subject-matter experts; not always peer-reviewed. Trace its claims to their sources.
Reference — A specialized encyclopedia or finding aid designed to orient you and direct you toward further sources. A starting point, not an endpoint.
I

Dropping the Bomb

On August 6 and 9, 1945, the United States dropped atomic bombs on Hiroshima and Nagasaki. Estimates of those killed range from roughly 110,000 to more than 200,000; deaths continued for months from burns, radiation sickness, and the near-total destruction of medical infrastructure. The uncertainty in the numbers is itself historically significant — and was, in part, a product of deliberate information management. The decision remains one of the most consequential and contested in American history. This section asks: What factors shaped Truman’s decision? Who was consulted, and who was not? What did American journalists and civilians know about the bombs’ effects, and when did they know it?

II

Japanese American Incarceration

Following the attack on Pearl Harbor, President Roosevelt signed Executive Order 9066 on February 19, 1942, authorizing the forced removal of more than 120,000 people of Japanese ancestry — roughly two-thirds of them American citizens — from the West Coast to ten incarceration camps in remote interior locations. A note on language: historians and affected communities have moved decisively toward “incarceration” rather than “internment,” which has a specific legal meaning distinct from mass forced removal. This section asks: What legal and political conditions made this possible? How did incarcerated people respond? What did resistance look like inside the camps?

Start Here
Reference Densho

Densho Encyclopedia

A peer-reviewed online reference work with nearly 1,500 articles by professional historians and community members, edited by Densho content director Brian Niiya. Covers all ten WRA camps, the major legal cases (Korematsu, Hirabayashi, Yasui, Endo), and significant individuals. Use it the way you’d use a specialized reference dictionary: to orient yourself in the vocabulary and chronology before moving into primary sources. As you read, notice the language choices: what terminology does this source use, and what does the introductory note about language tell you about the stakes of those choices?

III

Women at War & Work

World War II brought more than six million women into industrial and government jobs in the United States — the largest single shift in women’s labor-force participation in the nation’s history to that point. The federal Office of War Information actively recruited women through poster campaigns, radio spots, and magazine features. Then, once the war ended, it actively campaigned to return them to domestic roles. A note on the iconic image: the “We Can Do It!” Westinghouse poster now universally associated with Rosie the Riveter was not widely circulated during the war. It was produced for internal Westinghouse morale purposes. This disconnect between image and wartime reality is itself a research question worth pursuing. This section asks: What did wartime change actually mean for American women? Who was included in the “Rosie” narrative, and who was left out?

IV

African Americans at War & Work

More than one million African Americans served in the U.S. armed forces during World War II — across all branches and service roles, including labor and support battalions as well as combat units; the Smithsonian NMAAHC puts the figure at roughly 1.2 million — in a segregated military, under Jim Crow law, fighting for a nation whose founding documents declared all men equal. The Double V Campaign, launched by the Pittsburgh Courier in February 1942 following a letter from reader James G. Thompson, gave this contradiction a name: Victory abroad over fascism; Victory at home over American segregation. This section asks: What did Black military service mean in the context of domestic racial injustice? What role did the Black press play in documenting and demanding Black citizenship? What happened when veterans came home?

V

Knowledge of the Holocaust in the United States

The Holocaust — the systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of others by the Nazi regime and its collaborators — took place during the same years the United States was fighting World War II. The central question for this section is not simply what happened in Europe, but what Americans knew, when they knew it, and why rescue never became a national priority. This requires examining the structural conditions that shaped American responses: Depression-era isolationism, entrenched nativism and antisemitism, immigration restriction laws, the wartime media’s editorial choices, and the political calculations of the Roosevelt administration. The question “What did Americans know?” is inseparable from the question “What did knowing obligate them to do?”

General Resources

The resources below apply across all five research topics. Use them for orientation, cross-topic comparisons, and source discovery. A reminder about research process: professional historians use multiple source types — primary documents, secondary analyses, reference works, and institutional collections — and they triangulate. No single source, however authoritative, tells the whole story. Bring that same discipline to your research.

Reading the Cards: Source Types

Each card carries a colored bar and a label identifying what kind of source it is. Source type matters: it determines what a source can and cannot tell you, and how you should cite and weigh it.

Primary Source — Created at the time of the event: government documents, diaries, letters, newspapers of the period, oral histories, photographs. A primary source gives you direct evidence; it also carries the perspective and limits of its creator. Always ask: who made this, for what audience, and what might they have left out?
Secondary Source — Created after the event by someone interpreting primary sources: scholarly articles, textbook chapters, museum overviews, encyclopedia entries. Secondary sources give you context and analysis; they also carry the interpretive choices of their authors. Always ask: what argument is this source making, and what evidence does it use?
Curated Collection — A set of primary or secondary sources selected and organized by a library, archive, museum, or educational institution. Curation is an act of interpretation: the collector’s choices shape what you find. Always ask: who curated this collection, and what did they choose to include or leave out?
Longform — A substantial essay, magazine article, or work of narrative journalism that synthesizes evidence and makes an argument for a general educated audience. Not peer-reviewed in the same way as academic scholarship, but often written by subject-matter experts. Useful for understanding what a careful argument looks like; always check the author’s credentials and trace claims to their sources.
Reference — A specialized encyclopedia, database, or finding aid designed to orient you in a subject and direct you to further sources. Reference works are starting points, not endpoints. Always use a reference source to identify your next source, not as the final word on anything.
