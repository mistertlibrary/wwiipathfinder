✍️ Writing Assessment Questions

Create questions that truly test understanding, not just memorization

🎯 Bloom's Taxonomy: Levels of Thinking

Good assessment questions target different levels of thinking. Aim to include questions from at least 3-4 different levels:

6. Create (Highest Level)
Design, construct, develop, formulate, propose
Example: "Design a policy that might have prevented the Scramble for Africa. What obstacles would it face?"
5. Evaluate
Judge, critique, justify, assess, defend
Example: "Evaluate the effectiveness of the Meiji Restoration in protecting Japan from Western imperialism."
4. Analyze
Compare, contrast, examine, explain why
Example: "Analyze how economic inequality between Europe and Asia contributed to the Opium Wars."
3. Apply
Use, demonstrate, solve, apply concepts
Example: "Using the concept of 'civilizing mission,' explain how Europeans justified imperialism in Africa."
2. Understand
Explain, describe, summarize, interpret
Example: "Explain why the Suez Canal was strategically important to Britain."
1. Remember (Basic Level)
List, define, identify, recall, state
Example: "What year did the Berlin Conference take place?"

📝 Question Types: Good vs. Weak

Factual Recall

Tests basic knowledge. Use sparingly.

❌ Weak Example:
What was the Sepoy Rebellion?
Too vague, doesn't specify what kind of answer is expected
✅ Better Example:
Identify three causes of the 1857 Sepoy Rebellion against British rule in India.
Specific, tests understanding of causation

Compare & Contrast

Reveals deeper understanding of similarities and differences.

❌ Weak Example:
How were Japan and China different?
Too broad, lacks historical context
✅ Better Example:
Compare Japan's Meiji Restoration with China's Self-Strengthening Movement. Why did one succeed in modernization while the other failed?
Specific comparison with analytical component

Cause & Effect

Tests understanding of historical causation.

❌ Weak Example:
What caused imperialism?
Too general, no specific focus
✅ Better Example:
How did industrialization in Europe contribute to growing economic inequality between the West and colonized regions? Provide specific examples.
Clear causal relationship, asks for evidence

Source Analysis

Perfect for working with your primary source!

❌ Weak Example:
What does this source say?
Only tests reading comprehension
✅ Better Example:
Based on this treaty excerpt, what did European powers gain from the Berlin Conference? What does this reveal about their motivations for imperialism?
Connects source to broader themes

🧠 AP Historical Thinking Skills

Your assessment should align with AP skills your classmates are learning:

Contextualization

Connect events to broader historical conditions

"How did the Industrial Revolution create the economic conditions that led to the 'Scramble for Africa'?"
Causation

Explain why events happened

"What were the short-term and long-term causes of the Opium Wars?"
Comparison

Identify similarities and differences

"Compare British imperialism in Egypt with French imperialism in Algeria. What motivated each power?"
Continuity & Change

Trace what changed and what stayed the same

"How did the 'civilizing mission' ideology change European justifications for empire from earlier centuries?"

💡 Tips for Writing Effective Questions

  • Be specific: Instead of "Explain imperialism," try "Explain how Social Darwinism was used to justify European imperialism in Africa"
  • Avoid yes/no questions: These don't test deep understanding. Ask "how" and "why" instead
  • Use command words clearly: "Compare," "Analyze," "Evaluate" all mean different things
  • Test what you taught: Your questions should assess the main points from your lesson
  • Include a mix: 1-2 easier questions + 1-2 challenging ones gives everyone a chance to succeed
  • Connect to primary source: At least one question should reference the document you shared
  • Make it fair: Students should be able to answer based on your 15-minute lesson

✅ Sample Assessment for "Scramble for Africa"

Question 1 (Remember/Understand): What was the main purpose of the 1884-1885 Berlin Conference?

Question 2 (Apply/Analyze): Using the map we examined, explain how the Berlin Conference divided Africa without regard to existing ethnic or cultural boundaries. What problems might this create?

Question 3 (Analyze - Primary Source): Based on the treaty excerpt we read, what economic motivations did European powers have for claiming African territory? Cite specific evidence.

Question 4 (Evaluate): King Leopold II claimed his actions in the Congo were motivated by humanitarian goals. Based on what you learned today, evaluate the validity of this claim.
