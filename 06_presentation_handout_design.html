🎨 Designing Your Presentation & Handout

Create materials that engage your classmates and reinforce your teaching

⏱️ Remember: 15 Minutes Exactly

Plan your slides and handout to fit this timeframe

Too short = incomplete lesson | Too long = running over = points deducted

📊 Google Slides Presentation

Purpose: Visual support for your verbal teaching. Slides enhance understanding but shouldn't replace you!

Keep It Simple

Each slide should have ONE main idea. Use bullet points, not paragraphs. Your slides are visual aids, not your script.

Make It Visual

Use images, maps, charts, and graphs. A picture is worth a thousand words — especially in a 15-minute lesson.

Readable from the Back

Font size 24pt minimum. High contrast (dark text on light background or vice versa). No fancy fonts that are hard to read.

Limit Slides

For a 15-minute lesson: 8-12 slides maximum. That's about 1-2 minutes per slide including your explanation.

📄 Printed Handout

Purpose: A reference your classmates can study later. It should summarize key points and include visuals.

Well-Organized

Use clear sections with headings. Students should be able to scan and find information quickly.

Visually Appealing

Include 3+ images/graphics (REQUIRED). Use white space — don't cram everything together. Consider columns or boxes to organize content.

Complete but Concise

Cover all major points from your lesson. But keep it to 1-2 pages maximum. Focus on what's most important.

Properly Cited

Cite all images and any quotes. Include sources for major facts if taken from specific articles or books.

✅ Do's and ❌ Don'ts

✅ DO THIS

  • Start with a strong hook slide to grab attention
  • Use high-quality images relevant to your topic
  • Include maps showing geographic scope
  • Use bullet points, not full sentences
  • Add captions to images explaining their relevance
  • Use consistent colors and fonts throughout
  • End with a summary slide of key takeaways
  • Practice your timing with the slides

❌ DON'T DO THIS

  • Read directly from your slides word-for-word
  • Use tiny fonts that people can't read
  • Overload slides with too much text
  • Use distracting animations or transitions
  • Include random clip art that doesn't add value
  • Use low-resolution blurry images
  • Forget to cite your image sources
  • Make slides that could stand alone without you

📋 Pre-Presentation Checklist

  • Slides are clear, readable, and visually appealing
  • All images are cited on slides or handout
  • Handout includes 3+ images/graphics (REQUIRED)
  • Handout is well-organized with clear sections
  • Handout summarizes all major points from lesson
  • 7 copies of handout are printed and ready
  • Practiced presentation and it runs 15 minutes
  • Technology tested (slides load properly, etc.)
  • Hook/opening question prepared to engage class
  • Primary source document ready to share/discuss

💡 Pro Tips from Past Students

For Google Slides

  • Use the rule of thirds: Don't center everything; create visual interest with asymmetry
  • One powerful image > many small ones: Make visuals big and impactful
  • Dark mode option: White text on dark background can be easier to read in some classrooms
  • Speaker notes: Use them for your talking points so you don't forget key details

For Handouts

  • Create columns: Two-column layout makes handouts easier to read than wall-to-wall text
  • Use text boxes: Highlight key terms or dates in separate boxes for emphasis
  • Include timeline: If your topic spans years, a visual timeline helps
  • Add a "For Further Reading" section: Show where students can learn more

🎯 Sample Handout Structure

Page 1:

  • Title & Your Name at top
  • Hook Question: The big question you opened with
  • Background/Context: Brief paragraph setting the scene
  • Main Content: 3-5 key points with supporting details (use headers!)
  • Images: Strategically placed with captions
  • Key Vocabulary: Box with important terms defined
  • Timeline: If applicable to your topic
  • Primary Source Excerpt: Quote from document you discussed
  • Conclusion/Significance: Why this topic matters
  • Sources/Citations: At bottom or on back
